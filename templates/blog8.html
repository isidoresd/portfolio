08 Market Research

15/02/21

Whilst I have been analysing the difficulties someone with tetraplegia may face in their mobility, I have been examining some existing products that are currently avaible that aid with mobility. By doing so I aim to formulate design requirements for an assistive device.

The Quickie Q700, one of the more expensive wheelchairs at £21 000. This is most likely due to its impressive standing function, the chair extends upwards at an 85˚ angle lifting the sitter into a standing position. Not only does this allow them to access higher areas, but it alleviates pressure. There are six programmable positions available at the push of a button which enables further pressure alleviation.

The ID Soft features a high level of adjustability at a relatively low pricepoint although it is not powered. Backrest, seat angle and leg rest elevation are all easily adjustable by another user. The seat is deep and concaved with an adjustable headrest that would aid in helping posture.

The Ottobock A200 is listed as an indoor chair due to its narrow width. It is modular and foldable so it can easily be broken down and transported elsewhere. This is interesting as many of the chairs I have viewed are quite bulky and heavy and would likely require specialised transport for longer distances, this portability would allow for more freedom. There is a payoff for this however as the seat features less adjustability the other models.

The Kirton Duo is a not used for mobility but does feature posture adjustability functions that are worth recognising. In addition to the large amounts of padding, it has larger angles of adjustability meaning there are is greater range of positioning for pressure alleviation. The back of the chair is a rib like structure with a large range of adjustability which can support any posture. The chair is very large so would be unfeasable to be adapted into a wheelchair.

Dynamic cushions are sometimes used for people at medium to high risk of developing pressure ulcers. The linked model operates by alterneting the pressure across the cushion by inflating or deflating its sections whilst on a timer, therefore pressure is alternatingly reduced in a cycle.

Static pressure relief cushions are a cheaper alternative to dynamic ones as mention before. More likely recommended for use for people at lower risk of developing pressure ulcers. It works by spreading pressure evenly around the sitter using soft materials either in a gel or foam form which is the case for the product shown.
