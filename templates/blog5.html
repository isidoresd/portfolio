05 Exercise for tetraplegics

26/01/21

We are told very often just how much of an effect a sedentary lifestyle can have on ones health and general wellbeing. Numerous studies can attest to this. As Warren et al. highlight, men reported to spend more than ten hours a week driving are at an 82% greater risk of cardio vascular disease related mortality than those that use a car less than four hours a week. If physical inactivity can have such a drastic effects on the body, then how does someone with tetraplegia, who’s movement is severely impaired, minimise these effects?

In a 2009 study, it was found that for people with tetraplegia, hospital visits occurred more often and pain more severe for those also classified as obese (BMI greater than 30). There is clear impetus to maintain a healthy weight level, but this may be difficult for someone with tetraplegia.

After some research, it seems the primary mode of weight control is diet. According to the Spinal Injuries Association, it is fairly normal for dramatic weight loss to occur immediately after a spinal cord injury as the metabolism works hard to heal the body. But in the long term, due to the bodies inactivity and muscle loss, it is often all too easy to become overweight. Therefore it is often necessary for someone with tetraplegia to carefully control their diet.

Is it possible for someone with tetraplegia to exercise? Yes, but not so much in the sense that an able-bodies person exercises. Although tetraplegics may have some control of their upper body, this control is weak. Therefore, exercise for tetraplegics focuses on improving range of motion, referred to as Passive Range of Motion Exercises. These exercises consist of moving effected limbs in particular motions either individually or with the help of a partner. These exercises maintain flexibility and promote circulation with the goal of gradually gaining control back of these limbs.

During discussions with my project supervisor, we had discuss possibilities of designing a chair that enables exercise for tetraplegic afflicted persons. After reading related material, it may be difficult to replicate the type of exercise required with a device.
