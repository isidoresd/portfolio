01 Background

15/01/21

According to the National Health Service, there are 1.2 million wheelchair users in the UK alone, a striking 1.8% of the population. The International Spinal Cord Society estimates that as of 2019, at least 1200 new people suffer from some kind of spinal cord injuries every year with 35% of these causing teraplegia, a severe form of paralysis.

Tetraplegia (often reffered to as quadriplegia) is caused by damage to the spinal cord or brain known as lesions. Lesions can be the result of trauma, such as injury, diseases such as multiple sclerosis, or congenital disorders such as muscular dystrophy. The large majority of cases being caused by trauma according to the National Spinal Cord Injury Statistcal Center. Spinal injuries are categorised determined by where the spinal cord is damaged, the further up the spine the damage occurs, the higher the severity of the paralysis. Seperated into cervical segments, from C8 damage being the least severe, to C1 being the most. A person with C1 injuries require aid even to breathe whereas persons with C8 injuries are capable of using self-propelled wheelchairs.

A wheelchair is an essential aid for someone living with tetraplegia. By using them, someone with tetraplegia or any other form of paralysis or debilitating conditions, is able to live an active life. Wheelchairs are used extensively throughout the day and a user could be sitting in one for several hours at a time. This can cause problems as we will examine in a future blog post.

If you were to need a wheelchair, would you want it to look nice? Often when reading the news, one comes across articles on the latest developments in prosthesis. These prosthesis are often examples of exceptional design both in terms of its engineering and aesthetics. But when viewing wheelchairs, it becomes apparent that they do not recieve nearly as much design interest. Their design does not appear to have noticeably changed in decades. It is time that wheelchairs got an update.
