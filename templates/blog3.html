03 Tetraplegia

19/01/21

Tetraplegia is a paralysis of both the upper and lower body. This is opposed to paraplegia which refers to paralysis of the lower body only. It is one of the most severe forms of paralysis and results in the loss of sensation and movement in all four limbs as well as the torso although it can vary from person to person depending on where their spinal cord is damaged. This causes severe impairments to mobility and people with tetraplegia often need extensive care. The average lifetime cost of this care is estimated at around £1.87 million for someone with C grade injuries and above.

Tetraplegia currently has no cure however, treatment can help relieve some of the symptoms and can even sometimes help in regaining control over affected areas. It is caused by damage to the brain or spinal cord, both areas through which signals pass to muscles around the body to control movement. Briefly mentioned in my first post, a more indepth list of causes can is provided by Medical News Today which are listed as follows:

  • Falls and injuries.
  • Autoimmune conditions, such as multiple sclerosis.
  • Neurological conditions, such as stroke or cerebral palsy.
  • Tumors in the brain or spinal cord,
  • Infections of the spinal cord, such as polio
